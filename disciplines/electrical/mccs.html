MCCs

NEC Article 100 Definition: An assembly of one or more enclosed sections having a common bus and principally containing motor control units.

A MCC normally controls most of the motors in a given area of the plant and is located in a centralized location. A MCC, many times, is also used to feed small transformers, which in turn feed power panels, in the same area as the motors that it powers.

The enclosed sections of an MCC are commonly called “cubicles” or “buckets”. Each “bucket” can contain the following: main breaker or disconnect switch, motor starter, variable frequency drive (VFD), breaker, non-fused disconnect switch, fused disconnect switch, programmable logic controllers (PLCs), and metering equipment. “Buckets” can also contain, mounted to the outside front face, pushbuttons, indicator lights, local power/voltage/current metering, and other control/monitoring devices. “Buckets” usually contain a 120V or 24V control transformer/power supply.

MCCs are used for Low Voltage (50V to 1000V) and Medium Voltage (between 1000V and 35kV) in manufacturing plants, chemical plants, and refineries. MCCs are commonly used for three-phase loads, and the most common Low Voltage used for MCCs in the U.S. is 480V. Medium Voltage MCC voltages vary depending on the incoming power to the plant.

Power and control cables for each load originate from the MCC “bucket” for the given load. Control cables include local control, such as a hand-off-auto switch, Distributed Control System (DCS) cables, and Safety Instrumented System (SIS) cables.
